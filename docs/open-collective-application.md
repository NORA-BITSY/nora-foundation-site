# Open Collective Foundation — Application Draft

**Purpose of this document.** Copy-paste ready content for the Open Collective Foundation (OCF) application at <https://opencollective.com/foundation/apply>. OCF is a 501(c)(3) public charity that acts as fiscal sponsor for mission-aligned initiatives. Once approved, NORA Foundation collectives funds under OCF's 501(c)(3) umbrella; donations are tax-deductible; all transactions are publicly visible on the Open Collective ledger.

OCF's published requirements (as of April 2026):

1. The project must operate for charitable, educational, scientific, or public-interest purposes consistent with IRS §501(c)(3).
2. The project must commit to operating openly — public ledger of all inflows and outflows.
3. The project must not distribute profits to private individuals beyond reasonable compensation for services rendered.
4. OCF retains a ~10% fiscal-sponsorship fee on contributions received.

All copy below is written for the application form fields. Edit as needed before submission.

## Mission statement (short)

NORA Foundation publishes Canon — the public standard for verifiable reasoning artifacts — and develops Meridian-Canon, its reference implementation. Our mission is to give recipients of AI-produced reasoning (in legal, journalism, research, audit, and public-records contexts) a cryptographically-verifiable artifact they can falsify without cooperation from the issuer.

## Why this is charitable / public-interest work (longer)

Modern AI systems increasingly produce reasoning artifacts — retrievals, summaries, findings, synthesized briefs — that recipients are asked to rely on without structured means of auditing them independently. Under existing evidentiary standards (Federal Rules of Evidence 901 and 902; the *Daubert* admissibility framework; ACPO digital-evidence guidance), a methodology that cannot be falsified by the recipient does not support authentication. The practical consequence is a trust gap: AI-assisted retrieval and synthesis cannot be introduced as evidence in contexts where adversarial review is possible.

The gap is most acute in settings that the public depends on: courts administering family-, criminal-, and civil-justice matters where parties rely on large document corpora; investigative journalism that must defend every claim to its legal and editorial standards; academic research over private archives where reproducibility is the condition of publication; compliance and audit work governed by regulatory review; FOIA and transparency initiatives where both findings and intentional non-examinations matter.

Closing this gap requires a *public standard*, not a private product. If only one organization's implementation can produce verifiable artifacts, the verifiability is an illusion — the issuer becomes a trusted third party. NORA therefore publishes Canon under CC0 1.0 Universal, accepts amendments by public pull request, and actively seeds an ecosystem of independent verifiers across programming languages and institutional contexts.

Our work directly serves IRS §501(c)(3) purposes on multiple grounds:

- **Educational** — publishing a technical standard and accompanying documentation for the general public; producing tutorials, worked examples, and training materials for practitioners across law, journalism, and research.
- **Scientific** — advancing the state of the art in verifiable reasoning systems, information retrieval with provenance, and cryptographically-bound reasoning chains, with outputs published openly (specifications, reference implementations, evaluation methodologies).
- **Public-interest / civic-tech** — producing open infrastructure that strengthens the epistemic commons, particularly in evidentiary and journalistic contexts where independent verifiability is a precondition of public trust.

## Governance

At formation, NORA Foundation operates as a founder-led initiative with a commitment to widen governance alongside fiscal sponsorship. Specifically:

1. **Upon OCF approval**: a documented conflict-of-interest policy, a documented expense-approval policy, and a public ledger of all transactions via Open Collective.
2. **Within 90 days of OCF approval**: establishment of an advisory board (≥ 3 external members) drawn from the fields NORA serves — law, journalism, research, cryptography. Advisory-board members will not have fiduciary duties but will review material decisions and publish public advisory minutes.
3. **Within 12 months**: evaluation of whether standalone 501(c)(3) incorporation is warranted given scale. If yes, transition from OCF sponsorship to standalone status with a proper board of directors.

## Use of funds — specific commitments

Funds received through OCF will be used exclusively for:

- Drafting and stewardship of the Canon specification (editorial and technical-writing engagements).
- Development of the Meridian-Canon reference implementation (contractor engagements for specialized modules; cloud-compute costs; adversarial review).
- Grants to external parties implementing verifiers against the Canon standard in additional programming languages.
- Production of educational materials and worked examples for practitioner audiences.
- Administrative overhead consistent with OCF fiscal-sponsorship terms.

## How NORA aligns with OCF's charitable purposes

Open Collective Foundation's mission centers on supporting public-benefit technology initiatives that operate openly. NORA Foundation aligns on every axis:

- **Open** — specification under CC0; reference implementation under permissive OSI licence; all development in public repositories.
- **Public-benefit** — no commercial product; no licensing regime; no users behind a paywall; no coordination requirement with NORA for adopters.
- **Technology-for-accountability** — the entire work is about making AI systems auditable by the public, in the public, without private intermediaries.
- **Multi-stakeholder** — the standard is deliberately implementation-neutral; NORA actively seeds a plural ecosystem rather than concentrating verification under its own control.
